What is Project Management?

Project management is the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet project requirements.

Why is it Important?

  • Ensures Goal Achievement: Keeps projects aligned with organizational objectives
  • Optimizes Resources: Maximizes efficiency of time, budget, and team capabilities
  • Manages Risk: Identifies and mitigates potential problems before they escalate
  • Improves Communication: Facilitates clear coordination among stakeholders
  • Delivers Quality: Ensures deliverables meet defined standards and expectations

Project Management Frameworks

Waterfall

Sequential, phase-by-phase approach. Best for projects with well-defined requirements and minimal expected changes.

Agile

Iterative and flexible approach. Emphasizes collaboration, customer feedback, and rapid adaptation to change.

Scrum

Agile framework with sprints, daily standups, and defined roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, Team).

Kanban

Visual workflow management using boards and cards. Focuses on continuous delivery and limiting work in progress.

PRINCE2

Process-based method with defined stages, roles, and principles. Popular in UK and European organizations.

Lean

Focuses on maximizing value while minimizing waste. Emphasizes efficiency and continuous improvement.

Scrum Framework

Scrum in a Nutshell

The Scrum process is an iterative cycle that enables teams to deliver value incrementally

1

üìã Product Backlog Ordering

The Product Owner creates and prioritizes the Product Backlog, ordering items based on business value, risk, and dependencies. This living document contains all features, enhancements, and fixes needed for the product.

2

üéØ Sprint Planning

The Scrum Team collaborates to select a Sprint Goal and choose tasks from the Product Backlog to develop during the next Sprint (typically 1-4 weeks). The team commits to delivering these items by the end of the Sprint.

3

üîç Sprint Review

The Scrum Team and stakeholders inspect the increments created during the Sprint. The team demonstrates completed work, gathers feedback, and determines future adaptations for upcoming Sprints based on what was learned.

4

üîÑ Sprint Retrospective

The Scrum Team reviews their effectiveness, collaboration, and quality. They identify what went well, what could be improved, and make concrete adjustments for the next Sprint. The process then repeats, creating a continuous cycle of improvement.

üîÅ Continuous Cycle: Each Sprint builds on the lessons learned from previous iterations, enabling teams to deliver value consistently while continuously improving their processes.

Scrum Roles & Responsibilities

Three key roles work together to deliver successful products in Scrum

üìã

Product Owner

The "What" and "Why" - Maximizes Product Value

Key Responsibilities:

  • Owns the Product Backlog: Creates, prioritizes, and maintains all product requirements
  • Defines Product Vision: Communicates long-term goals and success metrics to the team
  • Stakeholder Management: Acts as the bridge between business stakeholders and the development team
  • Acceptance Criteria: Defines clear criteria for when work is considered "done"
  • Prioritization: Makes tough decisions on what features deliver the most business value
  • Available & Accessible: Answers team questions and provides clarification during the Sprint
üéØ

Scrum Master

The "How" - Facilitates the Scrum Process

Key Responsibilities:

  • Servant Leader: Serves the team by removing impediments and blockers
  • Process Guardian: Ensures the team follows Scrum principles and practices correctly
  • Facilitates Ceremonies: Organizes and leads Sprint Planning, Daily Standups, Reviews, and Retrospectives
  • Coaches the Team: Helps team members understand and implement Agile values
  • Shields the Team: Protects developers from external interruptions and distractions
  • Continuous Improvement: Identifies process inefficiencies and drives team improvements
üë®‚Äçüíª

Development Team (Developers)

The "Doers" - Build the Product Increment

Key Responsibilities:

  • Deliver Working Software: Create potentially shippable product increments each Sprint
  • Self-Organizing: Team decides how to accomplish the work without external direction
  • Cross-Functional: Collectively possess all skills needed (frontend, backend, QA, design, etc.)
  • Commitment: Forecast what can be completed and commit to Sprint Goals
  • Quality Ownership: Responsible for code quality, testing, and technical excellence
  • Collaborate Daily: Work together, share knowledge, and support each other to meet Sprint goals

üí° Important: All three roles are equal partners working toward a common goal. Success requires trust, collaboration, and clear communication between all team members.

Scrum Master vs Project Manager

Understanding the differences and similarities in software development

üîç Key Differences

üéØ

Scrum Master

  • ‚úì Servant Leader: Serves the team, removes blockers
  • ‚úì Process Focus: Ensures Scrum framework is followed
  • ‚úì No Authority: Doesn't assign tasks or manage people
  • ‚úì Facilitator: Runs ceremonies, coaches the team
  • ‚úì Team Empowerment: Helps team self-organize
  • ‚úì Single Team: Works with one Scrum team
  • ‚úì Agile Mindset: Champions Agile values and principles
  • ‚úì No Budget Control: Doesn't manage project finances
üìä

Project Manager

  • ‚úì Traditional Leader: Directs and controls the project
  • ‚úì Delivery Focus: Ensures project meets scope, time, budget
  • ‚úì Decision Authority: Assigns tasks, manages resources
  • ‚úì Planner: Creates timelines, manages dependencies
  • ‚úì Command & Control: Top-down management approach
  • ‚úì Multiple Projects: May handle several projects at once
  • ‚úì Methodology Flexible: Uses Waterfall, Agile, or hybrid
  • ‚úì Budget Owner: Controls and tracks project budget

ü§ù Similarities in Software Development

  • Stakeholder Communication: Both bridge technical teams and business stakeholders
  • Goal-Oriented: Focus on delivering successful project outcomes
  • Problem Solvers: Identify and resolve blockers and impediments
  • Team Coordination: Ensure collaboration between team members
  • Meeting Facilitation: Run regular team meetings and ceremonies
  • Progress Tracking: Monitor work completion and report status
  • Risk Management: Identify potential issues before they escalate
  • Quality Focus: Ensure deliverables meet required standards

üíº In Software Development Companies

Reality: Many software companies have Project Managers who adopt Scrum Master responsibilities or use hybrid approaches. Some organizations have both roles working together: the PM handles budget, timelines, and stakeholder management, while the Scrum Master focuses on team process and Agile coaching. In startups and smaller companies, one person often wears both hats.

Scrum Team Characteristics

The foundation of effective Scrum teams: structure, autonomy, and collaboration

üéØ

Self-Managed

The team internally decides who does what, when, and how. No external manager assigns tasks or dictates workflow. Team members autonomously organize their work to achieve the Sprint Goal.

üîß

Cross-Functional

Team members collectively have all skills required to create value each Sprint. This includes development, testing, design, DevOps, and any other expertise needed‚Äîno dependency on external teams.

ü§ù

No Hierarchies

All team members are equal. There are no titles or sub-teams within the Scrum Team. Everyone is simply a "Developer," regardless of their specialty (frontend, backend, QA, etc.). This promotes collaboration over competition.

üéñÔ∏è

Collective Accountability

The entire team is accountable for creating a valuable, usable Increment each Sprint. Success and failure are shared‚Äîno individual blame. The team wins together or learns together.

üë• Optimal Team Size

10 or Fewer People

The Scrum Team is typically 10 or fewer people. This size is small enough to remain nimble and large enough to complete significant work within a Sprint.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why This Matters:

  • Communication Overhead: Larger teams create exponentially more communication paths, slowing decision-making
  • Coordination Complexity: Too many people make Daily Standups, Sprint Planning, and Retrospectives unwieldy
  • Accountability Dilution: In larger groups, individual accountability becomes unclear
  • Agility Loss: Bigger teams move slower and adapt less effectively to change

üîÑ What to Do When Team Size Exceeds 10:

Solution: Split into Multiple Scrum Teams

  • Divide by Feature/Component: Create separate teams focusing on different product areas or features
  • Shared Product Backlog: All teams can work from the same Product Backlog managed by one Product Owner
  • Synchronized Sprints: Teams often align their Sprint timings for easier coordination
  • Scrum of Scrums: Representatives from each team meet regularly to coordinate dependencies and integration
  • Maintain Cross-Functionality: Each smaller team should still have all necessary skills to deliver value independently

üí° Remember: Small, empowered, cross-functional teams with shared accountability are the engine of successful Scrum implementations. Keep teams focused, autonomous, and properly sized for maximum effectiveness.

Scrum Event: The Sprint

üîÑ The Sprint

The heartbeat of Scrum - a fixed-length container for all other events

üìñ What is a Sprint?

A Sprint is a time-boxed iteration (typically 1-4 weeks, most commonly 2 weeks) during which the Scrum Team creates a potentially shippable product increment. Sprints are the container for all other Scrum events: Sprint Planning, Daily Scrums, Sprint Review, and Sprint Retrospective.

‚ú® Key Characteristics

  • Fixed Duration: Each Sprint has the same length throughout the project
  • Sprint Goal: Each Sprint has a clear objective that guides the team
  • No Gaps: A new Sprint starts immediately after the previous one ends
  • No Changes: Sprint scope is protected; no changes that endanger the Sprint Goal
  • Working Increment: Must produce a usable product increment by the end
  • Predictable Rhythm: Creates consistency and reduces complexity
  • Inspect & Adapt: Provides regular opportunities for feedback and course correction
  • Risk Mitigation: Short cycles limit risk to the Sprint duration

‚è±Ô∏è Typical 2-Week Sprint Timeline

Day 1 (Monday): Sprint Planning - Team plans the Sprint and creates Sprint Backlog
Days 2-9 (Tue-Thu, Week 2): Daily Scrums + Development Work
Day 10 (Friday, Week 2): Sprint Review - Demo increment to stakeholders
Day 10 (Friday afternoon): Sprint Retrospective - Team reflects and improves

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Sprint Rules

  • Cannot be extended: If work isn't finished, it returns to the Product Backlog
  • Can be cancelled: Only by the Product Owner if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete (rare)
  • Quality doesn't decrease: Standards are maintained regardless of remaining time
  • Scope can be clarified: Product Owner and Developers can negotiate scope as more is learned

üí° Key Insight: The Sprint creates a predictable rhythm that enables planning, limits risk, and ensures regular delivery of value. It's the foundation upon which all Scrum practices are built.

Scrum Event: Sprint Planning

üìã Sprint Planning

Setting the direction - defining what to build and how to build it

üìñ What is Sprint Planning?

Sprint Planning initiates the Sprint by laying out the work to be performed. The resulting plan is created by the collaborative work of the entire Scrum Team.

‚è∞
Time-box
Maximum 8 hours for a 1-month Sprint (proportionally less for shorter Sprints)
üë•
Attendees
Entire Scrum Team (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers)
üéØ
Outcome
Sprint Goal & Sprint Backlog

üìå Three Key Topics Addressed

1Ô∏è‚É£ Why is this Sprint valuable?

The Product Owner proposes how the product could increase its value and utility in the current Sprint. The whole Scrum Team then collaborates to define a Sprint Goal that communicates why the Sprint is valuable to stakeholders.

2Ô∏è‚É£ What can be Done this Sprint?

Through discussion with the Product Owner, the Developers select items from the Product Backlog to include in the current Sprint. The Scrum Team may refine these items during this process, which increases understanding and confidence. Selecting how much can be completed is up to the Developers.

3Ô∏è‚É£ How will the chosen work get done?

For each selected Product Backlog item, the Developers plan the work necessary to create an Increment that meets the Definition of Done. This is often done by decomposing Product Backlog items into smaller work items of one day or less, creating the Sprint Backlog.

‚úÖ Sprint Planning Best Practices

  • Come Prepared: Product Owner ensures the top backlog items are refined and ready
  • Review Velocity: Look at past Sprint performance to guide capacity planning
  • Set Realistic Goals: Don't overcommit; leave buffer for unknowns
  • Define "Done": Ensure everyone understands what "done" means
  • Break Down Work: Decompose large items into tasks the team can complete
  • Identify Dependencies: Surface blockers or external dependencies early
  • Craft a Clear Sprint Goal: Make it inspiring and focused
  • Get Team Buy-In: Ensure everyone commits to the plan

üéØ Sprint Goal Example: "Enable users to reset their passwords via email, improving account security and reducing support tickets by 30%."

Scrum Event: Daily Scrum

‚òÄÔ∏è Daily Scrum

The daily synchronization - keeping the team aligned and moving forward

üìñ What is the Daily Scrum?

The Daily Scrum is a 15-minute event for the Developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the Sprint Backlog as necessary. It's held at the same time and place every working day of the Sprint to reduce complexity.

‚è±Ô∏è
15 Minutes
Strictly time-boxed
üìÖ
Daily
Every working day
üìç
Same Place
Consistent location
üë®‚Äçüíª
For Devs
Others can observe

üéØ Purpose of Daily Scrum

  • Inspect Progress: Check advancement toward the Sprint Goal
  • Adapt the Sprint Backlog: Adjust planned work based on current reality
  • Identify Impediments: Surface blockers that need resolution
  • Promote Quick Decision-Making: Enable rapid problem-solving
  • Improve Communication: Keep everyone synchronized
  • Eliminate Other Meetings: Reduce need for additional status updates

üó£Ô∏è Common Structure (Flexible)

While the Developers can structure the Daily Scrum however they want, many teams use the classic Three Questions format:

1. What did I do yesterday that helped the team meet the Sprint Goal?
Share completed work and progress made
2. What will I do today to help the team meet the Sprint Goal?
State your plan for the day
3. Do I see any impediments that prevent me or the team from meeting the Sprint Goal?
Raise blockers and issues

Note: Teams can use other formats like walking the board, focusing on Sprint Goal progress, or discussing impediments first‚Äîwhatever works best for them!

‚ùå Common Anti-Patterns

  • Turning it into a status report to the Scrum Master
  • Going over 15 minutes regularly
  • Problem-solving during the meeting
  • Not focusing on the Sprint Goal
  • People showing up unprepared

‚úÖ Best Practices

  • Stand up to keep it short and focused
  • Use a timer to enforce the 15-minute limit
  • Take detailed discussions offline
  • Focus on the Sprint Goal and collaboration
  • Everyone comes prepared and engaged

üí° Remember: The Daily Scrum is for the Developers, by the Developers. It's not a status meeting for management‚Äîit's a planning and coordination event for the team.

Scrum Event: Sprint Review

üîç Sprint Review

Showcasing the work - inspecting the increment and adapting the backlog

üìñ What is the Sprint Review?

The Sprint Review is held at the end of the Sprint to inspect the Increment and adapt the Product Backlog if needed. The Scrum Team presents the results of their work to key stakeholders and discusses progress toward the Product Goal.

‚è∞
Time-box
Max 4 hours for 1-month Sprint (2 hours for 2-week Sprint)
üë•
Attendees
Scrum Team + Key Stakeholders
üéØ
Outcome
Updated Product Backlog

üìå What Happens in Sprint Review?

1Ô∏è‚É£ Review Sprint Results

The Scrum Team presents which Product Backlog items are "Done" and which are not. The team discusses what went well, what problems they encountered, and how they solved them.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Demonstrate the Increment

The Developers demo the working Increment to stakeholders. This is a live demonstration of the actual working software/product, not slides or mockups. Stakeholders can interact with it and provide immediate feedback.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Discuss the Product Backlog

The Product Owner discusses the current state of the Product Backlog and projects likely completion dates based on progress to date. The group collaborates on what to do next.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Review Market & Business Context

The group reviews how the marketplace or potential use of the product might have changed. This helps inform what's most valuable to do next.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Adapt the Product Backlog

Based on feedback and new insights, the Product Backlog may be adjusted to meet new opportunities. The team may identify new Product Backlog items or change priorities.

‚ú® Important Characteristics

  • Working Session: This is a collaborative working meeting, not a formal presentation
  • Informal: No PowerPoint slides‚Äîfocus on working software
  • Whole Team: The entire Scrum Team participates in the demo
  • Two-Way Dialogue: Encourages questions, feedback, and discussion
  • Actionable Feedback: Stakeholder input directly shapes future work
  • Demo What's Done: Only show completed, "Done" work
  • Celebrate Achievements: Acknowledge the team's hard work
  • Transparent: Be honest about what was and wasn't accomplished

üí° Sprint Review Best Practices

  • Prepare the demo environment in advance
  • Use real data, not dummy/fake data
  • Let different team members demo different features
  • Invite the right stakeholders who can provide valuable feedback
  • Start with the Sprint Goal and what was achieved
  • Encourage hands-on interaction with the product
  • Capture feedback and new ideas immediately
  • Don't demo incomplete work or use slides

Scrum Event: Sprint Retrospective

üîÑ Sprint Retrospective

Continuous improvement - reflecting on process and planning improvements

üìñ What is the Sprint Retrospective?

The Sprint Retrospective provides an opportunity for the Scrum Team to inspect itself and create a plan for improvements to be enacted during the next Sprint. It occurs after the Sprint Review and before the next Sprint Planning.

‚è∞
Time-box
Max 3 hours for 1-month Sprint (1.5 hours for 2-week Sprint)
üë•
Attendees
Scrum Team ONLY (private session)
üéØ
Outcome
Actionable improvement items

üéØ Purpose & Focus Areas

The Scrum Team inspects how the last Sprint went with regards to:

üë•
Individuals

Team dynamics, collaboration, morale

‚öôÔ∏è
Processes

Workflows, ceremonies, practices

üîß
Tools

Technology, equipment, infrastructure

üìã Typical Retrospective Structure

1Ô∏è‚É£ Set the Stage (5 min)

Create a safe environment. Review the retrospective goals and ground rules. Remind everyone this is a blameless, constructive session.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Gather Data (15-20 min)

Collect facts about the Sprint. Common formats: What went well? / What didn't go well? / What can we improve? Or use techniques like Timeline, Sailboat, Starfish, etc.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Generate Insights (20-30 min)

Analyze the data together. Look for patterns, root causes, and underlying issues. Use techniques like 5 Whys, affinity mapping, or dot voting to prioritize.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Decide What to Do (20-30 min)

Create 1-3 concrete action items for the next Sprint. Assign owners and define clear success criteria. More than 3 items often means nothing gets done!

5Ô∏è‚É£ Close the Retrospective (5 min)

Summarize action items and owners. Appreciate the team's participation. Optional: gather feedback on the retrospective itself (meta-retro).

‚úÖ Best Practices

  • Psychological Safety: Create a blameless culture
  • Rotate Formats: Use different retrospective techniques
  • Follow Through: Review previous action items first
  • Limit Improvements: Focus on 1-3 actionable items
  • Make it Fun: Use games and creative exercises
  • Track Progress: Document improvements over time

‚ùå Common Anti-Patterns

  • Skipping retrospectives when "nothing went wrong"
  • Using the same format every time (gets stale)
